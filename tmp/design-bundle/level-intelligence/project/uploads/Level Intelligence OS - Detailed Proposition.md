# Level Intelligence OS - Detailed Proposition

## Executive Summary

Level Construction already has the hard part: trusted relationships, commercial construction judgment, development instincts, hospitality experience, capital discipline, and a practical sense for which opportunities are worth time.

The opportunity is to turn that judgment into a private operating system that Level owns.

**Level Intelligence OS** is a Level-specific construction intelligence and pursuit system. It helps Level find project signals earlier, qualify them through Level's own operating criteria, route work to the right person, coordinate RFQs and go/no-go decisions, preserve relationship memory, and give leadership a weekly view of what matters.

This is not a generic dashboard, chatbot, CRM migration, or Procore replacement. It is a working operating layer around Level's business development, preconstruction, project pursuit, development, hospitality, and capital workflows.

Mercator is a useful category reference: it shows that construction firms can benefit from early project intelligence. The Level-specific opportunity is broader. **Mercator finds opportunities. Level Intelligence OS finds, qualifies, routes, and converts opportunities using Level's own operating judgment.**

The recommended first step is a focused 90-day pilot:

- consolidate active opportunities, accounts, contacts, RFQs, and market signals;
- build Level-specific qualification scoring;
- create an RFQ and go/no-go command center;
- establish relationship and pursuit memory;
- route approvals and next actions;
- generate a weekly executive intelligence brief;
- and define the next integration roadmap from evidence, not assumptions.

The near-term outcome is cleaner visibility and faster decisions. The long-term outcome is a proprietary operating memory that improves every time Level reviews a signal, pursues an opportunity, wins a job, passes on a poor-fit project, or learns from execution.

## The Business Problem

Level's advantage is operational, but much of that advantage still lives in places that software cannot easily use:

- emails and text threads;
- RFQ packages and attachments;
- bid notes and scope clarifications;
- permit comments and jurisdiction updates;
- spreadsheets and project folders;
- meeting notes and phone calls;
- individual relationship memory;
- executive judgment about fit, risk, timing, and margin.

That knowledge is valuable, but it is fragile. It is difficult to search, difficult to hand off, difficult to compare across opportunities, and difficult to improve systematically.

The result is not a lack of effort. It is a lack of one operating layer that can answer basic leadership questions quickly:

- Which opportunities are active right now?
- Which are worth pursuing, watching, or passing on?
- Which RFQs are due soon, incomplete, or under-reviewed?
- Which relationships need follow-up?
- Which project signals are early enough to act on?
- Which decisions are waiting on leadership?
- Which assumptions keep repeating across wins and losses?
- Which existing tools are helping, and which are creating friction?

Level Intelligence OS is designed to create that operating layer.

## Proposed Positioning

**A private construction intelligence operating system for Level.**

It combines four capabilities in one Level-owned workflow:

| Capability | What It Does | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Find | Detects public and private project signals before they become obvious | Level sees more opportunities earlier |
| Qualify | Scores opportunities using Level's criteria, history, relationships, and capacity | Leadership spends less time on poor-fit work |
| Route | Assigns owners, next actions, approvals, and follow-ups | Good opportunities do not stall in inboxes |
| Convert | Supports RFQs, go/no-go memos, pursuit briefs, and executive reporting | Level turns intelligence into action |

The central message is simple:

> Level Intelligence OS turns Level's construction judgment into a repeatable, searchable, decision-ready operating system.

## What The System Does

### 1. Opportunity Intelligence

The system creates a signal inbox for early project and relationship movement.

Potential signal sources include:

- permit filings;
- planning commission and city council agendas;
- zoning, rezoning, and variance activity;
- title transfers and ownership changes;
- commercial real estate news;
- hospitality and franchise expansion announcements;
- broker notes and relationship tips;
- inbound RFQs;
- CRM activity;
- email threads and meeting notes;
- internal pursuit updates.

Each signal becomes a structured record:

- source and date;
- account, owner, developer, brand, operator, or contact;
- site, parcel, market, and jurisdiction;
- likely project type;
- estimated timing;
- relationship path;
- confidence level;
- recommended owner;
- recommended next action.

This gives Level an earlier, cleaner view of potential work without forcing every signal to become a full opportunity too soon.

### 2. Level-Specific Qualification Engine

The system should not use a generic opportunity score. It should encode Level's own decision logic.

Example qualification dimensions:

- geography and market fit;
- asset type fit;
- project size and timing;
- owner/developer quality;
- relationship strength;
- brand or operator relevance;
- entitlement and permit complexity;
- financing credibility;
- construction margin potential;
- internal capacity;
- prior Level experience;
- vendor and subcontractor fit;
- competitive risk;
- strategic value.

Each opportunity receives:

- pursue, watch, or pass recommendation;
- confidence level;
- reasoning;
- missing information;
- known risks;
- next action;
- recommended internal owner;
- comparable historical work where available.

The goal is not to remove judgment. The goal is to put better context in front of the people making the judgment.

### 3. Relationship Intelligence

Relationship memory is one of Level's most important assets. The system makes it searchable and actionable.

It should answer:

- Who do we know at this account?
- Who owns the relationship internally?
- When was the last meaningful touch?
- What have we built, bid, financed, or discussed with them?
- Which opportunities are tied to this person or company?
- What is the next useful follow-up?
- Who can make a warm intro?
- What should leadership know before a call?

Relationship intelligence becomes part of the opportunity record, not a separate CRM chore.

### 4. RFQ And Go/No-Go Command Center

RFQs are high-friction because the critical details arrive in language-heavy packages: emails, PDFs, attachments, deadlines, scopes, addenda, and requirements.

Level Intelligence OS should:

- ingest RFQs and attachments;
- summarize scope and requirements;
- extract deadlines and submission format;
- identify missing documents;
- flag insurance, bonding, site, or schedule issues;
- compare the opportunity to Level's fit criteria;
- draft a go/no-go memo;
- assign internal tasks;
- track clarifications and addenda;
- record pursuit decision and outcome.

This creates better selectivity and faster review. Level should not just bid more. It should bid better.

### 5. Permit And Entitlement Awareness

For development-heavy or preconstruction-sensitive pursuits, the system can track jurisdictional risk.

Potential functions:

- track permit or entitlement status;
- summarize plan review comments;
- identify who owes the next response;
- calculate days in review;
- compare timing against jurisdiction memory;
- draft internal follow-up notes;
- flag blockers that affect pursuit or schedule assumptions.

This is especially useful when project attractiveness depends on timing, approvals, or hidden municipal friction.

### 6. Executive Operating View

The system should produce a weekly leadership brief that is more useful than a status meeting.

The brief should show:

- top new signals;
- active opportunities by stage;
- highest-scoring pursuits;
- RFQs due soon;
- stalled opportunities;
- pending approvals;
- relationship follow-ups;
- permit or entitlement risks;
- changes since last week;
- decisions needed from leadership;
- lessons learned from wins, losses, and passes.

The leadership view should be exception-oriented. Executives should see what changed, what is stuck, what needs judgment, and who owns the next move.

## Operating Model

Level Intelligence OS should work as a set of specialist workflows under one governance model.

| Workflow | Role |
|---|---|
| Signal Scout | Finds, dedupes, summarizes, and routes project signals |
| Qualification | Scores opportunities against Level-specific criteria |
| Relationship | Builds account/contact briefs and follow-up context |
| RFQ/Bid | Extracts requirements, deadlines, risks, and go/no-go memos |
| Permit/Entitlement | Tracks jurisdiction status and hidden timing risk |
| Executive Briefing | Turns system activity into a weekly decision brief |
| Finance/Capital | Connects opportunity intelligence to financing, underwriting, and investor context where relevant |

This structure keeps the system practical. It does not ask one vague assistant to know everything. Each workflow has a clear job, defined inputs, and defined authority.

## Human Control And Trust

The system should prepare work, not silently make consequential decisions.

| Authority Level | System Can Do | Human Control |
|---|---|---|
| Autonomous | classify signals, summarize RFQs, dedupe contacts, draft briefs | Logged for review |
| Suggest | recommend owner, next action, pursue/watch/pass, follow-up draft | Human can accept or edit |
| Approval Required | external outreach, bid submission, vendor award, budget change, investor communication | Explicit approval required |
| Manual | legal, contract, financing, final strategic decisions | Human executes |

Every meaningful recommendation should include:

- what the system recommends;
- why it recommends it;
- what evidence supports it;
- what is missing;
- confidence level;
- approver;
- decision;
- outcome.

This creates an audit trail and lets the system learn from leadership overrides.

## Pilot Plan

### Pilot Objective

Prove that Level Intelligence OS can improve opportunity visibility, qualification discipline, RFQ coordination, executive reporting, and institutional memory within 90 days.

### Pilot Scope

The v1 pilot should include:

- active opportunity pipeline;
- account and contact relationship layer;
- signal inbox;
- Level-specific qualification scoring;
- RFQ intake and go/no-go memo workflow;
- approval and next-action queue;
- weekly executive brief;
- outcome capture and feedback loop.

### Timeline

| Period | Focus | Client-Visible Deliverable |
|---|---|---|
| Weeks 1-2 | Foundation | Data model, roles, active opportunities, account/contact views, executive dashboard skeleton |
| Weeks 3-5 | Intelligence | Signal inbox, opportunity summaries, qualification score, relationship briefs, first weekly digest |
| Weeks 6-8 | Workflow | RFQ command center, deadline extraction, go/no-go memo, approval queue, bid task tracking |
| Weeks 9-12 | Operating rhythm | Weekly executive brief, outcome tracking, adoption review, phase-two roadmap |

### Success Metrics

Pilot success should be measured by operating usefulness, not novelty.

- 100 percent of active pursuits visible in the system.
- Each pursuit has owner, stage, source, score, next action, and age.
- At least 10 useful signals captured, reviewed, and routed.
- RFQs tracked with deadlines, requirements, and go/no-go status.
- Weekly executive brief generated from live system data.
- Leadership identifies faster decisions, fewer blind spots, or clearer accountability.
- Phase-two roadmap is approved, adjusted, or rejected based on evidence.

## Phase Two Options

After the pilot, expansion should be based on where Level sees the highest value.

Potential phase-two modules:

- permit and entitlement monitoring;
- email, calendar, and CRM integration;
- Procore or construction-management read-only sync;
- vendor and subcontractor performance memory;
- project handoff from pursuit to execution;
- finance and capital workflow;
- investor reporting support;
- hospitality pipeline tracking;
- software rationalization and existing-tool usage audit.

Procore should be treated carefully. The recommended position is not "replace Procore." The stronger position is: use Level Intelligence OS to see what work actually needs Procore, what can be handled elsewhere, and where Level can reduce friction or cost with evidence.

## Commercial And Ownership Principles

The final commercial structure should be decided after discovery, but the operating principles should be clear.

### Engagement

90-day pilot to design, build, test, and operationalize Level Intelligence OS v1.

### Pilot Deliverables

- Level data model and workflow map;
- active opportunity/account/contact database;
- signal inbox and triage workflow;
- qualification scoring framework;
- RFQ intake and go/no-go memo workflow;
- approval queue;
- weekly executive brief;
- pilot success report;
- phase-two roadmap.

### Ownership

Recommended principles:

- Level owns its data, operating memory, domain model, and workflow definitions.
- Level should retain export rights for pilot data and generated records.
- Code ownership and licensing should be defined in the final agreement.
- AI model providers should remain replaceable infrastructure.
- The strategic asset is Level's operating judgment made structured and reusable.

### Data And Confidentiality

- Level data is confidential.
- Role-based access and audit logs should be required.
- No external reuse of Level data without written permission.
- Sensitive actions require human approval.
- Data export should remain available.

## Discovery Questions

The pilot should begin with a short discovery process focused on actual workflow, not abstract AI strategy.

Key questions:

- Where do new opportunities originate today?
- Who owns opportunity qualification?
- What makes a Level opportunity attractive or unattractive?
- Where do RFQs live, and how are go/no-go decisions made?
- What fields does leadership need to see every week?
- What relationships, markets, and asset types matter most?
- Which systems are in use today: CRM, Procore, email, calendar, accounting, storage?
- What data can be imported safely for the pilot?
- Which actions require explicit approval?
- What would make the pilot obviously worth continuing?

## Closing Position

Level Intelligence OS should be presented as a serious operating asset, not an AI experiment.

The first version gives Level one place to see opportunities, understand fit, track RFQs, preserve relationship memory, and route decisions. The longer-term version becomes Level's private construction intelligence layer: a system that learns from Level's signals, relationships, decisions, wins, losses, and operating judgment.

That is the core proposition:

> Level should not rent generic intelligence when it can build the operating memory that makes its own team sharper.
